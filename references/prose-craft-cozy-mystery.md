# Prose Craft Rulebook — Cozy Mystery

Genre variant of the default craft rulebook (`references/prose-craft.md`). Same inclusion rule: only when `voice/style-guide.md` is absent, chosen when the project's genre reads as cozy / amateur-sleuth / small-town mystery. Ports the StoryStormer app's `prose-cozy-mystery` prompt (v1.0.0).

---

## Voice and Warmth

The narrative voice in cozy mystery reads like a letter from a perceptive friend — someone who notices everything, judges gently, and finds genuine pleasure in the texture of daily life. This voice earns the right to guide the reader through darker material precisely because it has first established warmth, humor, and trustworthiness. Write sentences that feel spoken aloud, carrying the cadence of a storyteller settling into a favorite chair, not performing for an audience but sharing because the story is too good to keep.

Warmth lives in specificity. Not "she made tea" but "she filled the blue-rimmed pot her grandmother had carted across three state lines, the one with the hairline crack that wept if you poured too fast." The cozy voice lingers on objects that carry history, meals that carry memory, routines that carry identity. Every domestic detail is also characterization — what a person keeps in their kitchen tells you who they are before a single line of dialogue.

Humor arrives through observation rather than setup-and-punchline. The narrator notices the absurd gap between what people say and what they mean, between how the town presents itself and how it actually operates, between the gravity of murder and the persistent mundanity of life continuing around it — someone still needs to pick up the dry cleaning, the cat still demands breakfast at six. Let comedy emerge from the friction between the extraordinary event and the ordinary world it has invaded.

## Sensory Comfort and Place

The setting in cozy mystery functions as a character the reader wants to visit. Ground every scene in at least two sensory details drawn from the domestic and natural world: the yeasty exhale of bread dough left to rise, the particular weight of autumn light through leaded glass, the crunch of gravel under tires on a lane that hasn't been paved since the seventies. Food is never merely consumed — it is prepared, smelled, shared, and through its preparation, relationships are revealed. A character who rolls pasta by hand processes grief differently than one who orders delivery.

Weather participates in mood but never melodramatically. Fog doesn't "creep ominously" — it settles over the harbor the way it always does in October, and the fact that this October someone is dead makes the familiar fog newly strange. Seasons mark time more reliably than calendars: the reader tracks the investigation through the ripening of garden tomatoes, the shortening of afternoons, the shift from iced tea to hot cider.

Small-town geography matters. The reader should be able to draw a rough map — the bakery is three doors down from the post office, the victim's house sits at the end of Birch Lane where the road turns to dirt. Spatial relationships create narrative possibility: who could see what from where, who would have passed whom on the way, which route avoids the main street.

## Clue-Planting and Misdirection

The best clues hide in plain sight, embedded in details that serve double duty — they are interesting as characterization or setting detail on first reading and become significant evidence on re-reading. A character's habit of always sitting facing the door reads as quirky specificity the first time; after the reveal, it reads as hypervigilance born of guilt. Plant at least three genuine clues per investigation chapter, each disguised as something the narrator would notice anyway: a peculiar choice of words, an object slightly out of place, a reaction that's a beat too slow or a beat too fast.

Misdirection works through character rather than plot mechanics. Red herrings shouldn't feel manufactured — they should emerge from the genuine complexity of people who all have secrets, most of which have nothing to do with the murder. The town gossip's evasiveness about Tuesday evening turns out to involve a clandestine romance, not a crime. The victim's business partner's anxiety stems from embezzlement they feared the victim had discovered, not violence they committed. Every false lead should satisfy when revealed, adding texture to the community rather than feeling like a cheat.

Observational detail does the heaviest lifting. The amateur sleuth notices things because noticing is who they are — a baker registers that the sugar bowl has been moved, a librarian catches a misattributed quotation, a gardener recognizes soil on someone's shoes that doesn't match their claimed whereabouts. The detective's expertise is their ordinary life, and the ordinary becomes investigative.

## Dialogue as Community

Cozy mystery dialogue is often the engine of the narrative — characters talk their way through the investigation, and conversations carry as much weight as action sequences do in other genres. People in small communities speak in layers: the surface topic, the thing they actually want to discuss, and the thing they're afraid someone else will bring up. Three women discussing the church fundraiser are simultaneously processing the murder, evaluating each other's alibis, and protecting their own secrets.

Dialogue tags in cozy mystery can do more work than "said" alone. Characters speak "over the rim of a teacup," "while scrubbing the already-clean counter," "without looking up from the crossword." The accompanying action grounds the conversation in domestic life and simultaneously reveals emotional states — the compulsive cleaning, the refusal of eye contact, the performance of normalcy.

Give each recurring character a verbal signature that the reader comes to recognize and enjoy: the neighbor who begins every sentence with "Well, I'm not one to say, but—," the sheriff who speaks exclusively in understatement, the victim's mother who corrects everyone's grammar even while grieving. These signatures become part of the cozy world's texture, little pleasures that reward the returning reader.

## Pacing the Investigation

Cozy mysteries breathe between tension beats. After a discovery that advances the investigation, allow a scene of ordinary life — a baking sequence, a walk with the dog, a conversation about something unrelated to the crime — before the next revelation. This rhythm mirrors the amateur detective's life: they cannot pursue the mystery full-time because they have a business to run, a cat to feed, a friendship that needs tending. The investigation weaves through life rather than replacing it.

Chapter endings in cozy mystery pull the reader forward without cliff-hanging into melodrama. A new question raised, a small contradiction noticed, an invitation accepted that the reader suspects will lead somewhere interesting — these are the quiet hooks that keep pages turning at the pace of pleasurable curiosity rather than anxious urgency. The reader should want to know what happens next the way they want the next course of a good meal, not the way they want an alarm to stop ringing.

Revelations arrive through conversation more often than through action. The critical piece of information comes from the chatty postal worker, the overheard exchange at the farmer's market, the offhand remark over dinner that suddenly connects two previously unrelated facts. The cozy detective solves crimes by being embedded in community — by knowing people well enough to notice when something is off.
