# Prose Craft Rulebook — Romance

Genre variant of the default craft rulebook (`references/prose-craft.md`). ## Emotional Interiority

Romance lives inside the body. The point-of-view character's emotional landscape is the primary terrain of every scene, more important than plot, setting, or dialogue — not because these elements don't matter, but because the reader experiences them through the filter of what the protagonist is feeling. Interiority in romance is not navel-gazing; it is the mechanism through which external events acquire meaning. A thunderstorm is just weather until the protagonist stands in it remembering the last time they were caught in rain with the person they're trying to forget. Then the storm becomes story.

Layer physical sensation beneath every emotional beat. Anxiety doesn't announce itself as "she felt anxious" — it arrives as a tightening behind the sternum, a metallic taste at the back of the tongue, the compulsive need to check the phone that hasn't buzzed. Attraction manifests in hyper-awareness: suddenly conscious of the exact distance between her arm and his on the shared armrest, the heat radiating from his skin like a challenge, the way his laugh travels through the floorboards when she's one room away. The body knows before the mind admits, and the reader should feel that lag between physical recognition and intellectual acceptance.

Vulnerability earns its moments by being rare. If the protagonist bares their soul every chapter, exposure loses its charge. Build toward vulnerability through accumulation — small moments of guarded honesty, near-misses where the truth almost surfaces, conversations that circle the real subject without landing on it. When the walls finally come down, the reader should feel the structural shift. Something that was holding the character upright is no longer there, and they're freestanding for the first time, terrified and exhilarated.

## Tension Through Denial

What characters refuse to do generates more erotic and emotional tension than what they do. The almost-kiss that doesn't happen because one of them pulls back. The hand that hovers over the other person's shoulder and then drops. The loaded silence after a confession that the other person doesn't acknowledge. These withheld moments create an ache in the reader that consummation eventually resolves — but only if the denial has been specific, embodied, and costly enough to feel earned.

Physical proximity scenes are choreographed with the precision of dance. Track the geometry: where hands are, where eyes land, who moves closer and who holds still, the shrinking space between bodies that becomes the most charged real estate in the scene. *"His hand was on the counter, three inches from hers. She could feel the warmth of it without looking, the way you feel sunlight through a window even with your eyes closed."* Proximity is a negotiation conducted in centimeters, and the reader should feel every one.

Internal resistance has to be real and specific — not manufactured conflict but genuine reason. A character who won't allow themselves to fall in love because "they might get hurt" is generic. A character who won't allow themselves to fall in love because the last time they trusted someone completely, that person used their secrets as leverage in a custody battle — that resistance is load-bearing. The reader needs to feel that the emotional walls exist for survival, not for plot convenience, so that watching those walls come down feels like witnessing genuine courage.

## Dialogue With Subtext

Romance dialogue operates on at least two levels simultaneously. The surface conversation concerns the ostensible topic — work, a shared project, a practical problem to solve. Beneath it, the real exchange is happening: a negotiation of power, desire, trust, and vulnerability that neither character will name directly. *"You didn't have to bring the files yourself."* Surface meaning: the email would have sufficed. Subtext: you chose to come here, to stand in my doorway, and we both know why.

Banter is a romance dialect — it establishes intellectual compatibility, creates pleasure in the characters' company, and generates tension through the intimacy of play. Good banter has escalation: each line raises the stakes slightly, pushes the boundary a little further, tests whether the other person will follow. The moment one character says something genuinely revealing in the middle of what they thought was safe territory — that's the crack in the banter where the real feelings show through. Write toward those cracks.

Vulnerability in dialogue arrives as incompleteness. The sentence that starts and doesn't finish. The admission that comes out sideways: "It's not that I don't want to — it's that I don't know how to want something this much and also survive losing it." Romance characters are often most honest when they're most afraid, and their speech patterns should reflect the struggle between the impulse to confess and the instinct to protect.

## Sensory Emphasis

Touch is the primary sense in romance. Not just the obvious touches — kisses, embraces, the choreography of intimacy — but the incidental ones that accumulate meaning: a hand steadying an elbow on stairs, the brush of fingers when passing a coffee cup, the press of a shoulder against a shoulder in a crowded room. Each touch should land with specific tactile detail. Not "he touched her face" but "his thumb traced the edge of her jawline, and his hand was rougher than she expected, calloused in ways that made her wonder what he built with those hands when no one was watching."

Temperature and atmosphere carry emotional weight. Rooms get warmer when tension rises — or the character becomes aware of warmth that was already there. Cold is isolation, vulnerability, the absence of the other person. The specific quality of air in a shared space — whether it feels charged or easy, thick or crystalline — mirrors the emotional dynamic of the scene. A summer evening that won't cool down and a conversation that won't resolve are the same kind of pressure.

Scent is the most intimate sense and should be deployed at the moments of closest proximity. Not cologne or perfume (too expected) but the real, human details: the smell of coffee on someone's breath during an early-morning confession, soap and cedar on a jacket borrowed against the cold, the salt of skin at the hollow of a throat. Scent bypasses intellectual processing and arrives directly as emotion — use it for the moments where rational thought is exactly what the character is losing.

## Anti-Patterns

Purple prose that names emotions rather than embodying them — "desire coursed through her veins," "passion ignited in his chest," "she felt a surge of longing" — tells the reader what to feel rather than making them feel it. Replace every instance of "she felt [emotion]" with the physical, sensory, behavioral evidence of that emotion. The reader should diagnose the feeling before the character names it.

Rushed emotional escalation destroys the architecture that makes romance satisfying. Characters who go from strangers to soulmates in three chapters haven't earned the reader's emotional investment. Each stage of increasing intimacy — from awareness to attraction to vulnerability to commitment — needs its own scenes, its own setbacks, its own small victories. The reader should feel that every inch of emotional ground was hard-won.

Consent exists as a foundation, not an obstacle. Characters who read each other's signals, who check in through action and dialogue, who pause when they sense hesitation — these behaviors are not pace-breakers but tension-builders. The moment of "is this okay?" asked with genuine uncertainty is more emotionally charged than any impulsive gesture, because it makes visible the gap between desire and permission, between wanting and being wanted back.
