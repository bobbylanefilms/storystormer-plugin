## Sentence-Level Craft

The sentence is the primary unit of pleasure in literary fiction. Each sentence should reward close reading with its rhythm, its precision, its small surprises of juxtaposition and emphasis. Craft periodic sentences that delay gratification, holding the reader in syntactic suspension — subordinate clause nesting within subordinate clause, the landscape of the sentence's meaning assembling itself in fragments — until the main verb arrives and the accumulated tension resolves into clarity. Then follow with a short sentence. Three words. Let the contrast do the work.

Parallel construction, deployed across the span of a passage rather than within a single sentence, creates thematic architecture the reader feels before they understand it. A character described through three actions in the first chapter — *"She sorted the mail by postmark date. She alphabetized spices she never used. She ironed pillowcases."* — is being defined by pattern, and when the pattern breaks later, the break carries narrative force. The reader doesn't need to consciously register the parallel; the disruption still lands because rhythm teaches expectation and variation teaches meaning.

Avoid the elegant-variation trap — the compulsion to find a synonym rather than repeat a word. In literary fiction, repetition is a tool, not a failure. When the right word appears three times in a paragraph, each occurrence should carry a slightly different weight, the meaning shifting through context the way a musical theme shifts through key changes. "She was tired. Tired of the argument, tired of being right about it, tired of the particular silence that followed being right." The repetition isn't laziness — it's accumulation.

## Metaphor Rooted in Character

Metaphor in literary fiction must emerge from the specific consciousness through which the narrative filters. A mathematician perceives the world through patterns, symmetry, and the beauty of proof — their grief might manifest as an equation that won't balance, a series that diverges when it should converge. A gardener experiences time through growing seasons and the patience of perennials — their loneliness might arrive as a garden gone to seed, not through neglect but through the absence of anyone to share the harvest. The metaphor reveals the character as much as the thing being described, because the comparison a person reaches for is itself a form of self-portrait.

Resist metaphors that explain. The best figurative language creates a small mystery — a momentary cognitive displacement that asks the reader to hold two unlike things together and feel the current that passes between them. *"Her marriage was a house with the windows painted shut."* This doesn't explain the marriage; it gives the reader a physical sensation — airlessness, a sealed perimeter, the faint panic of enclosure — that the analytical mind then processes into meaning. Let metaphors be experiential first and interpretable second.

Extend a metaphor no further than its natural life. One precise image held for a sentence or two creates resonance. The same image forced through a full paragraph becomes a conceit that draws attention to its own cleverness. Literary fiction's relationship to figurative language should feel like a jazz musician's relationship to theme — reference it, riff on it, let it color the passage, but don't play it so insistently that the audience hears only the device and not the music.

## Egri's Social Dimensions

Every character in literary fiction occupies a social position that shapes their perception, their diction, and their understanding of what is possible. Lajos Egri's three dimensions — physiological, psychological, and sociological — are not a checklist but a lens. The sociological dimension is most often under-written: a character's class, education, profession, religion, and cultural context should be visible in how they see the world, not just stated in their backstory.

A character who grew up in scarcity reads abundance differently than one who grew up in privilege — the former notices price tags, exit routes, the quality of other people's shoes; the latter notices aesthetic choices, comfort, the signal a room sends about its owner. Neither character thinks about their own social conditioning. It simply operates, shaping what they notice and what they ignore, what they find threatening and what they find banal. Write from inside these conditioned perceptions without explaining them; the reader will assemble the sociology from the accumulated evidence of the character's attention.

Diction carries class the way an accent carries geography. A character's internal monologue uses the vocabulary available to them — not dumbed down for characters of limited education, but shaped by what they've been exposed to. A dock worker who reads poetry on lunch breaks has a richer internal vocabulary than his spoken register would suggest; that gap between how he thinks and how he speaks is itself a characterization opportunity that reveals the social forces constraining his self-expression.

## Accumulation Over Escalation

Literary fiction builds toward its effects through accumulation — the slow layering of detail, observation, and interaction that creates a felt sense of a life — rather than through the escalating crises of plot-driven fiction. This doesn't mean nothing happens. It means that what happens acquires weight from what has come before, and the reader's emotional response is proportional to the depth of their immersion in the character's world. A divorce that arrives on page 200 after 199 pages of a marriage slowly revealed in all its particular textures is devastating in a way that a divorce on page 10 cannot be, regardless of how well it's written.

The small event that reveals a large truth is literary fiction's signature move. A woman rearranging the silverware drawer while her husband explains why he needs to take the job in another city. A father teaching his daughter to parallel park on the morning she leaves for college. The reader understands that the scene is not about silverware or parking; it is about control, about the physical performance of normalcy while the ground shifts beneath it. These domestic moments carry structural weight precisely because they refuse to announce their importance.

Resist the pressure to provide revelation, epiphany, or resolution at the end of every chapter or section. Literary fiction earns the right to end on irresolution — a character still uncertain, a situation still unresolved, a question still open. The reader's discomfort with that irresolution is itself the point. Life does not organize itself into satisfying narrative arcs, and literary fiction's willingness to honor that messiness is what distinguishes it from genre fiction's comfort of closure.

## Irony as Structural Principle

Irony in literary fiction is not sarcasm, cleverness, or detachment. It is the structural awareness that the reader knows something the character doesn't, or that the character knows something they cannot act on, or that the narrative reveals a pattern the characters are too close to see. Dramatic irony — the reader watching a character walk toward a disaster they could prevent with information the reader possesses — generates the distinctive literary-fiction feeling of compassionate helplessness.

Situational irony emerges from the gap between intention and outcome, between what characters plan and what actually unfolds. A character who moves across the country to escape their family's influence and ends up recreating the same dynamics with new people. A character who builds an elaborate system of control to prevent exactly the catastrophe that the system itself eventually causes. These ironies are not twists — they are recognitions, moments where the narrative pattern becomes visible and the reader understands something about the human tendency to enact what we most fear.

Tonal irony — the coolness of the prose working against the heat of the subject matter — is literary fiction's most powerful register. A passage about terrible grief written in measured, precise, almost clinical language creates a tension between content and form that makes both more powerful. The restraint suggests a consciousness that cannot afford to feel fully, a character maintaining composure at enormous cost, and the reader supplies the emotion that the prose will not permit itself to express directly.

## Anti-Patterns

Plot-driven pacing that sacrifices reflection for momentum — the breathless chapter-ending cliffhanger, the twist that arrives before the reader has fully inhabited the scene — belongs to genres optimized for propulsion. Literary fiction's pacing allows the reader to dwell, to re-read a passage, to sit with an image or a revelation before the narrative demands their attention again. When a scene needs time, give it time. The reader who has committed to literary fiction has agreed to a slower metabolism.

On-the-nose symbolism — the character standing at a crossroads while deciding between two paths, the broken mirror reflecting a fractured identity, the storm arriving with the argument — insults the reader's intelligence by doing their interpretive work for them. If a symbol feels too neat, too available, too easily decoded, it has failed. The best symbols in literary fiction emerge from the specific material conditions of the story and surprise even the writer — a recurring image that gathers meaning through context rather than through the writer's deliberate placement.

Thematic statement masquerading as character thought — a character who conveniently articulates the novel's theme in their internal monologue — collapses the distance between author and character that literary fiction depends on. Characters do not know what their stories are about. They are too busy living inside them. The theme emerges from the pattern of events, choices, and consequences that the reader perceives from outside — the author's job is to arrange the evidence, not to deliver the verdict.
