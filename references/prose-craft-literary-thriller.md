# Prose Craft Rulebook — Literary Thriller

Genre variant of the default craft rulebook (`references/prose-craft.md`). Same inclusion rule: only when `voice/style-guide.md` is absent, chosen when the project's genre reads as thriller / suspense / action-crime. Ports the StoryStormer app's `prose-literary-thriller` prompt (v1.0.0).

---

## Sentence Construction

Vary sentence openings deliberately and aggressively. Default prose gravitates toward subject-first construction; resist this pull. In descriptive and tension-building passages, open sentences with participial phrases, temporal clauses, absolute constructions, and prepositional phrases at least as often as you open with the grammatical subject. Never allow more than two consecutive sentences to begin with the subject or a pronoun.

Front-load participial phrases to lead with sensation before grounding the reader in the main clause: *"Sitting on the floor in front of the closet, he selected a pair of shoes that would not betray him with squeaks or slips."* Embed participial phrases mid-sentence for continuous motion: *"The aircraft shuddered, bucking and shimmying like a wounded animal, while passengers gripped their armrests."* Stack participials when accumulating sensation demands it, piling clause upon clause until the main verb arrives as release: *"Gasping for breath, heart pounding, wrenched painfully from side to side in her harness, Lindsey hoped for a tree, a rock outcropping, anything that would halt their fall."*

Sentence fragments are powerful precisely because they are short. Deploy them for dramatic moments and pacing control — one to eight words that land like a fist. "She was pissed." "TWANG!" "Murder." "Down. All the way down." These impact sentences interrupt the flow of complex prose, creating contrast that makes both the fragment and the surrounding sentences more effective.

Use em dashes sparingly: no more than two or three per scene. Before reaching for an em dash, consider whether a semicolon, a comma, a period creating two shorter sentences, or a sentence fragment would serve the moment better. Em dashes are powerful precisely because they're rare; overuse flattens their impact.

Eliminate uncertainty indicators — "trying," "maybe," "seemed to," "almost" — that dilute narrative authority. Characters don't try to open doors. They open them, or they fail.

## Vocabulary and Register

Elevated but comprehensible words carry the most weight, deployed sparingly so they land with force rather than creating a fog of ornamentation. Prefer "pellucid" over "clear," "gossamer" over "thin," "preternatural" over "unusual" — but only when the moment earns it. Favor kinetic verbs that create motion and energy over static or linking constructions: "bulleted," "rocketed," "careened," "wallowed," "bucked and shimmied."

Shift register to match context. Elevated language suits philosophical moments and interiority; technical precision suits professional sequences and tactical thinking; colloquial directness suits stress responses and characters pushed past their limits. Characters under pressure speak and think differently than characters at ease — their vocabulary contracts, their syntax fragments, their profanity surfaces.

Layer sensory vocabulary across multiple channels rather than defaulting to the visual. Auditory detail — "shriek," "paradiddle," "cacophonous" — places the reader inside the soundscape. Tactile detail — "viscous," "gelid," "serrated" — makes surfaces real under the hand. Kinesthetic detail — "careening," "plummeting," "undulating" — puts the body in motion. Each scene's sensory palette should feel specific to its setting and circumstances, not interchangeable with any other scene.

## Technical Precision

Exact measurements ground fictional reality and create measurable tension. "Eighteen-inch hole," "forty thousand pounds of thrust," "Impact in five minutes." A precise count — "147 passengers plus 4 flight attendants" — carries weight that "many will die" cannot. Numbers create dread because they make consequences countable.

Weave professional expertise through character competence rather than exposition. Knowledge emerges through action: a pilot recognizing a sound, a detective reading a crime scene, a mechanic diagnosing a failure by feel. Let expertise drive problem-solving attempts and open narrative pathways. Show tactical and diagnostic thinking patterns — damage evaluation, threat analysis, probability calculation — as the character's natural mode of processing crisis.

Then systematically strip those professional advantages away. Training becomes useless against unprecedented circumstances. Expertise cannot overcome impossible physics. The competence that made the character formidable becomes the measure of how dire the situation has become.

## Cinematic Description

Think of scenes cinematically, composing prose the way a director composes shots. Wide establishing shots open with long sentences, multiple prepositional phrases, and layered visual planes that convey the geography of the moment — the reader needs to see the whole terrain before entering it. Medium shots draw closer, focusing on character-environment relationships and spatial dynamics, the camera settling on the people within the landscape. Close-ups zero in on crucial objects, expressions, and tension points, rendered in precise sensory detail: individual beads of sweat forming across a forehead, the tremor in a hand, the flicker of recognition crossing a face. Tracking shots follow action through space with participial phrases and continuous motion verbs, pulling readers along the trajectory as it unfolds.

Paragraph breaks serve as cuts. Ellipses as fades. Fragmented sentences as montage. Viewed from above, characters appear vulnerable; from below, threatening. Unbalanced perspectives breed disorientation — and disorientation breeds dread.

## Time Manipulation

Stretch crisis seconds into paragraph-length detail, weaving simultaneous reactions through cause-effect chains so that a moment of impact or terror expands into something the reader experiences at the speed of adrenaline rather than the speed of the clock. Use countdown pressure — "Impact in X minutes" — while allowing narrative content to expand far beyond the stated interval. Employ hypnotic repetition to extend brief moments into eternal sensation: "Rolling, rolling, their momentum dropping."

Let characters recognize the distortion themselves. Time warps under the pressure of proximity to death, and a character's awareness of that warping makes it visceral rather than merely structural. Present tense fragments breaking through past narrative show trauma activation and expand subjective time.

Condense routine hours to paragraphs. Expand crisis microseconds to detailed sequences. The contrast between compressed calm and dilated crisis is itself a pacing tool.

## Dialogue

Rapid-fire exchanges collide with longer speeches that build toward emotional crescendos. "What?" "Nothing." "What nothing?" Characters interrupt and build on each other's thoughts — people under stress don't wait for others to finish, don't yield the floor politely, don't let a sentence land before lobbing back a complication.

Beneath professional conversation, personal stakes simmer. Surface discussion concerns work, logistics, strategy, while the real tensions involve loyalty, power, fear, desire. Technical jargon grounds dramatic moments in authenticity while the emotional subtext runs beneath like a current.

Wit serves as emotional armor. Characters deflect serious topics with humor precisely when they are most vulnerable, using intelligence as a shield against feeling. Escalations build rapidly, characters complicating each other's points until reaching climactic revelation — and then, after the verbal sparring, one moment of genuine vulnerability lands with maximum impact because the armor has been lowered.

Diagnostic dialogue — characters reasoning through technical problems aloud — creates both competence and authenticity. When the moment demands it, emotional truth cuts through complexity like a blade.

## Character Psychology

Present inner thoughts in *italics*, letting them provide humorous counterpoint to spoken words or running commentary on experience. The gap between what a character says and what they think is one of the richest seams in close-perspective fiction. During intense action or sustained dialogue, limit interiority to maintain focus on the immediate — the reader should feel urgency, not reflection.

Professional training conflicts with overwhelming circumstances; show this tension as it builds. Characters retreat to technical comfort zones as psychological anchors during chaos — obsessive focus on precise measurements and technical details becomes a control mechanism when agency slips away, a way to impose order on situations that have none. Backstory surfaces through technical recognition patterns and professional experience rather than explicit recollection. A character doesn't remember their training; they enact it, and the enactment reveals who they were before this moment.

## Atmosphere and Setting

Setting participates. Exterior landscapes trap, hunt, and betray. Weather becomes predator; terrain becomes conspirator. Interior spaces carry their own menace: claustrophobic cabins where walls press closer with each mile, corridors that swallow sound and direction, familiar rooms turned sinister through context. Environment mirrors psychological states — cramped spaces amplifying paranoia, vast spaces amplifying vulnerability, the familiar turned strange amplifying dissociation.

Craft metaphors rooted in the specific setting rather than reaching for generic figurative language. Each environment should generate its own imagery, drawn from its particular textures, geometries, and sensory character. A desert scene produces desert metaphors. A cockpit produces mechanical ones. The setting teaches you how to describe it.
