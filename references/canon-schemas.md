# Canon Schemas

Detailed schemas for Canon assets — character bios and worldbuilding entries. The skills (`character-bio`, `worldbuilding-entry`) operate the workflow; this file specifies the *content shape* of what they produce.

For framework vocabulary (McKee, Truby, Egri, Weiland, Voice Fingerprint, Thematic Posture, structural frameworks, worldbuilding tiers + categories) see `references/frameworks.md`. For file layouts (frontmatter, snapshots, directory structure) see `references/file-schemas.md`.

------

## Character Bio Schemas

Each tier has a target word count, a hard cap, and per-section budgets. The budgets are guidance, not gates — but if you're consistently exceeding them, trim before adding. Density matters as much as coverage; downstream prose agents do better with a sharper bio than with a sprawling one.

### Major Bio (~1,400w standard; ~1,700–1,900w Extended)

**Hard cap: 2,000 words. Do not exceed.**

Extended Major is permitted for POV protagonists, primary antagonists, and characters carrying the story's thematic load. The extra ~300–500 words go to specific sections (Voice & Mannerisms, Psychology, Background) — not to padding existing material.

| # | Section | Standard | Extended | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Quick Reference | ~70w | ~70w | Structured key-value. Omit unknown fields rather than fabricate. |
| 1 | Essential Identity | ~60w | ~60w | Prose paragraph. Irreducible truth + arc one-liner. No restating Quick Reference. |
| 2 | Physical Presence | ~150w | ~150w | Include sensory anchors beyond visual (smell, sound entering a room, tactile signature). |
| 3 | Voice & Mannerisms | ~250w | ~400w | Voice Fingerprint sub-fields (`frameworks.md`). Extended adds Humor register + Inner Voice. |
| 4 | Psychology & Personality | ~230w | ~450w | Manifestation prose, not type labels. Extended adds Thematic Posture + Grief/trauma register. |
| 5 | Background & Family | ~160w | ~280w | The Ghost lives here. Extended adds Object Attachments + Sense Memory. |
| 6 | Social Context & Lifestyle | ~150w | ~150w | Egri's three dimensions. Class, education, religion, politics. |
| 7 | Story Role & Relationships | ~150w | ~150w | Moral argument: what this character represents thematically. |

If approaching the cap, cut from Physical Presence duplication and Social Context flourishes before touching Voice, Psychology, or Background.

#### Section content guidance

**0. Quick Reference** — structured key-value, the only section that breaks the prose rule. Fields: Name + aliases, Age, Occupation, Story Role, Living Situation, Relationship Status, MBTI (4-letter), Enneagram (type with wing → integration → disintegration paths, e.g. `6w5 → integrates to 9, disintegrates to 3`), Clifton Strengths (top 5), The Lie (one sentence). Omit fields you don't know — never fabricate.

**1. Essential Identity** — purely prose insight. The irreducible truth about who this person is, then one sentence on who they become. Do not restate facts from Quick Reference.

**2. Physical Presence** — what readers experience when this character enters a scene. Height, build, distinguishing features (scars, tattoos, birthmarks), clothing and grooming, one signature visual detail. Then 2–3 sensory anchors *beyond the visual*:
- **Smell** — cologne, motor oil, antiseptic, nothing at all.
- **Sound entering a room** — heavy boots, jingling keys, silence.
- **Tactile signature** — rough hands, cold handshake, vice grip, feather-light touch.

**3. Voice & Mannerisms** — physical presence in dialogue scenes (posture, walking style, 1–2 signature gestures, laugh/smile type, emotional visibility), then the Voice Fingerprint vectors from `frameworks.md`: Register & rhythm, Reference well, Profanity profile, Humor register, Verbal tell under pressure, Never-says, Inner Voice (Extended Major / POV characters), Audience shift. Close with truth/deception relationship and their tell when lying.

**4. Psychology & Personality** — *manifestation prose*, not type labels (labels live in Quick Reference). One or two concrete sentences each on:
- **MBTI manifestation** — how this type shows up in this specific character's behavior and decisions.
- **Enneagram manifestation** — concrete integration/disintegration. Who they become healthy vs stressed.
- **Clifton Strengths manifestation** — how their top strengths shape their approach to problems and relationships.
- **Behavior under pressure** — fight / flight / freeze / fawn, concretely. *"Fight: Dara doesn't raise her voice — she goes quiet, precise, and surgically cruel."*
- **Core motivation** — Want vs. Need. The Want usually serves the Lie; the Need points toward truth.
- Biggest fear, what they're most proud of, key values and moral lines.

For Extended Major: add **Grief/trauma register** (how grief physically manifests — distinct from general stress response — what happens to sleep, appetite, hands, voice) and **Thematic Posture** (when the story has a defined thematic axis — a stance *produced by* the character's psychology, not imposed).

**5. Background & Family** — where they grew up, key childhood memories, family composition and current relationships, 1–2 defining moments, biggest regret or trauma. **The Ghost** (Weiland): the specific past event that created the Lie. Link it explicitly to the Lie. For Extended Major: 2–4 **object attachments and sense memories** the prose agent will need for grief, flashback, or trauma scenes — a specific piece of handwriting, a smell, a ritual object, a piece of furniture, a song. Name them, and name the memory each is attached to.

**6. Social Context & Lifestyle** — Egri's three dimensions in prose:
- **Class and socioeconomic position** — where they fall, and how aware they are of it.
- **Education level and attitude** — formally educated or self-taught? Do they value learning, resent academia, feel excluded from it?
- **Religious beliefs** — devout, lapsed, atheist, complicated. How it affects their choices.
- **Political outlook** — what they believe about how society should work. What would they march for, or refuse to?
- Hobbies, skills, competencies outside their primary role. Relationship with money only as it reveals character.

**7. Story Role & Relationships** — primary relationships with other characters, narrative function, key internal/external conflicts, how the arc connects to theme. **Moral argument** (Truby): what this character represents thematically. What position in the story's moral debate do they embody?

### Supporting Bio (~700w)

**Hard cap: 770 words.**

| # | Section | Budget | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Quick Reference | ~40w | Compact: Name/Age/Occupation/Role, Enneagram (with wing), The Lie (if applicable). |
| 1 | Essential Identity | ~40w | Prose paragraph. Arc may be modest or absent — be honest about scale. |
| 2 | Physical Presence & Sensory Signature | ~100w | Most distinctive features + 1–2 sensory anchors beyond visual. |
| 3 | Voice & Mannerisms | ~150w | Voice Fingerprint at supporting scale: Register/rhythm, Reference well, Profanity profile, Never-says (1–2). |
| 4 | Psychology & Inner World | ~130w | MBTI manifestation (one sentence), Enneagram (paths in 2–3 sentences), Want vs Need, Under pressure. |
| 5 | Background | ~80w | Essential history only. Ghost if applicable. |
| 6 | Social Context | ~80w | All four Egri dimensions in 1–2 sentences each. No conditional dimensions. |
| 7 | Story Function | ~100w | Key relationships, narrative role (mentor/foil/etc.), conflicts, irreducible contribution. |

Bias toward Supporting when ambiguous with Major — easier to upgrade later than to prune. If approaching the cap, trim Story Function and Background first; Psychology and Voice carry the most weight.

### Minor Bio (~350w)

**Hard cap: 400 words.**

| # | Section | Budget | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Identity & Role | ~50w | Function in the story. No arc summary — minor characters typically don't change. |
| 2 | Physical Presence | ~80w | Most distinctive features only + one non-visual sensory anchor + one signature visual detail. |
| 3 | Voice | ~90w | Compressed Voice Fingerprint: register/rhythm, profanity habit, optional Never-says line. |
| 4 | Core Drive & Type | ~70w | Enneagram (no wing needed), what they want in their scenes, behavior under pressure. No MBTI, no Lie, no Ghost. |
| 5 | Story Function | ~60w | Relationship to leads, what they bring. Include Social Context only if scene-visible. |

Bias toward Supporting when ambiguous with Minor — a Minor bio is fast but too thin for recurring characters. If approaching the cap, trim Story Function first.

## Worldbuilding Entry Schemas

Worldbuilding entries come in two formats: a short **Reference Note** for real-world elements with story-specific modifications, and a longer **Full Entry** for invented elements. See `frameworks.md` § Worldbuilding for the tier framework and the 14 categories.

### Reference Note (~200–300w)

For real-world elements with story-specific significance. Four sections, all prose:

1. **Real-World Baseline** — what this element is in the real world, 1–2 sentences. Establish the reference point the model already knows.
2. **Story-Specific Modifications** — what's different, customized, or unique here. The protagonist's Camaro has a hidden compartment. The real city has a fictional neighborhood.
3. **Sensory Details Unique to This Story** — recurring sensory motifs the prose agent should use. The car always smells like cigarettes and gun oil. The neighborhood is perpetually foggy.
4. **Consistency Constraints** — facts that override real-world knowledge, or story-specific rules. State as clear, unambiguous rules.

### Full Entry — Universal Sections (all categories)

Every Full Entry has these four sections:

- **Core Identity** — what is it, what defines it, what is its role in the story. First thing a prose agent needs.
- **Sensory Palette** — what it looks, sounds, smells, feels, and (if relevant) tastes like. Every category has a sensory signature: weapons have sound (click of safety, ring of steel), technology has feel (engine warmth, vibration), magic has smell and sensation (ozone, pins-and-needles), locations have atmosphere, creatures have presence (breathing, fur or scales).
- **Consistency Constraints** — hard rules the prose agent must NEVER violate. State as "always / never / exactly." Quantify where possible. Example: *"The magazine holds exactly 8 rounds — never describe reloading after 8+ shots without a reload scene."*
- **Story Role** — significance to plot and characters. When this element appears, who interacts with it, what it means for the story.

### Full Entry — Category-Specific Sections

Add as relevant to the category:

- **Physical Characteristics** — locations, objects, vehicles, weapons, creatures. Appearance, size, materials, layout, anatomy.
- **Rules and Mechanics** — magic systems, technology, social systems. How it functions. What's possible / not. Costs, limitations, prerequisites, internal logic.
- **Interaction Experience** — weapons, magic, technology, vehicles, locations. What it feels like to use or experience from a character's perspective.
- **Failure Modes** — weapons, magic, technology, vehicles, locations. What can go wrong and how. Critical for dramatic scenes.
- **History and Origins** — especially lore, organizations, locations. Only include history that matters for the story.
- **Cultural / Social Context** — locations, lore, organizations, social systems, objects. How characters and society view this element.
- **Leadership and Structure** — organizations only. Chain of command, internal politics, formal vs actual power.
- **Behavioral Patterns** — creatures, autonomous magic. Migration, territorial behavior, feeding, autonomous manifestations.

### Length Guidelines by Category (Full Entries)

| Category | Word Count | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Objects (simple, story-significant) | 300–400w | Light touch — just what's unique and how it feels. |
| Locations (fictional) | 500–700w | Sensory palette, layout, atmosphere, interaction rules. |
| Geography | 400–600w | Scale, climate, terrain, ecology, natural hazards. |
| Vehicles | 400–500w | Sensory details, handling, failure modes. |
| Weapons (fictional) | 400–500w | Interaction feel, failure modes, sensory signature. |
| Technology (fictional) | 500–700w | Rules, limitations, sensory experience, social implications. |
| Magic systems | 700–1000w | Full rules, costs, limitations, sensory experience, failure modes. |
| Creatures | 400–600w | Physical description, behavior, threat level, interaction patterns. |
| Organizations / factions | 500–700w | Identity, structure, goals, perception, internal tensions, reach. |
| Culture (customs, daily life) | 400–600w | How it works, who benefits, what's transgressive, daily life impact. |
| History (eras, events, figures) | 400–600w | Key figures and events, living memory vs distant past, how it's remembered. |
| Religion | 500–700w | Doctrine, practice, devotion gradient, folk vs official practice. |
| Languages | 300–500w | Who speaks it, register, speech patterns, untranslatable concepts. |
| Lore (general mythology / knowledge) | 400–600w | Enough for a prose agent to write characters who carry this knowledge. |

### Category-Specific Guidance

Beyond the universal and section-by-section guidance above, each category has its own texture:

- **Organization** — make it feel like a living entity with its own personality and internal contradictions. Add public-perception-vs-reality, internal tensions (factions, generational divides, ideological disagreements), and resources/reach.
- **Location** — sense of place a prose agent can render into immersive scenes. Atmosphere and mood; what regulars see that newcomers miss.
- **Geography** — scale and spatial relationships, climate and seasonal character, natural hazards.
- **Object** — provenance (where it came from, prior owners) and significance to specific characters.
- **Vehicle** — handling quirks (what regulars know that first-timers don't), maintenance realities (what breaks, what's expensive).
- **Weapon** — skill requirements; reload/maintenance rhythm (how it punctuates combat scenes).
- **Technology** — access and stratification (who has it, who doesn't), user-experience curve.
- **Magic** — Rules and Mechanics must be *exhaustive*. Who can use it and how it's learned; caster vs bystander experience. Failure Modes carry unusual weight — spell failure and backlash are frequent dramatic beats.
- **Creature** — threat level and triggers; intelligence level (instinctive animal, cunning predator, sapient being); habitat; cultural significance (revered, feared, hunted, domesticated, mythologized).
- **Culture** — who benefits and who suffers; what's considered transgressive; how characters navigate it (accept, resist, exploit, subvert).
- **History** — living memory vs distant past; key figures and events characters invoke; how the past is remembered (honored, suppressed, mythologized, disputed).
- **Religion** — devotion gradient (devout to lapsed to hostile); ritual and practice; doctrine vs folk practice.
- **Languages** — who speaks it and their social status; register and dialect; speech patterns that reveal origin; untranslatable concepts. Sensory Palette includes the language's *sound* — its music and texture as characters hear it.
- **Lore** — transmission (oral, written, ritual, hidden); who knows it and who does not; how it affects current behavior.

## Writing Style & Consistency Rules

These rules apply to every Canon asset — character bios and worldbuilding entries alike.

### Voice and detail
- Present tense for descriptions; past tense for backstory.
- Flowing prose under section headers. Not bullet points, not key-value pairs. The one exception is the Quick Reference block at the top of Major and Supporting bios.
- Specific and concrete, not abstract. Details should *reveal* character or world, not just describe surface. *"She has a nervous habit of twisting her wedding ring, even though she's been divorced for three years"* — useful. *"She has brown hair"* — useless.
- Sensory beyond visual. Sounds, smells, textures, temperatures, physical sensations.
- Describe how elements change under different conditions (lighting, weather, time of day, use, stress).
- Sensory details from a character's perspective, not omniscient narrator's.
- Avoid clichés; find fresh angles.
- Match the genre's tone — noir for crime, lyrical for literary, period-appropriate for historical.

### Examples of good details
- *"She has a nervous habit of twisting her wedding ring, even though she's been divorced for three years."*
- *"His laugh is surprisingly high-pitched for someone so intimidating — a squeak that embarrasses him."*
- *"Under pressure, he doesn't yell — he goes perfectly still and starts speaking in complete, grammatically flawless sentences, which terrifies everyone who knows him."*
- *"She says 'I'm fine' the way other people say 'leave me alone.'"*
- *"Reaches for military-history analogies under pressure; would never use a sports metaphor. Never says 'I feel.'"*
- *"The sword's blade shifts color in moonlight, revealing runes that pulse with faint blue luminescence. It weighs just under three pounds — light enough for extended combat but heavy enough that each strike carries authority."*
- *"Drawing the Veil costs the caster their body heat — after sustained casting, breath fogs and fingertips go numb. Failed attempts produce a sharp ozone smell and leave the caster with a migraine lasting 1–3 hours."*

### Avoid
- Generic descriptions: *"She has brown hair."*
- Telling without showing: *"He's funny."*
- Exhaustive physical inventories.
- Vague worldbuilding: *"The magic is powerful but has costs."* — say what costs, name the limits.
- **Reference-well overlap across major characters** — if three of four main characters reach for military history, none of them has a distinctive reference well.

### Consistency-constraint writing (worldbuilding)
- State constraints as clear, unambiguous rules.
- Use "always," "never," "exactly" where appropriate.
- Include both what IS true and what is NOT true when the distinction matters.
- Quantify where possible (exact counts, distances, durations).

### Consistency checks (all asset types)
- Descriptions align with primer, treatment, and manifest mentions.
- For character bios: backstory supports the character's story actions; the Ghost logically produces the Lie.
- For worldbuilding: consistency constraints don't contradict each other or other Canon entries.
- If new content conflicts with existing Canon, existing Canon is authoritative — adjust the new asset.

### Voice Fingerprint cross-check (characters)
Scan main-cast Voice Fingerprints as a set. Reference wells should not substantially overlap. Profanity profiles should vary. Each character's Never-says should be specific to that character, not a generic template.

### Inner Voice differentiation (Extended Major bios, multi-POV stories)
Verify each POV character's interior monologue is meaningfully distinct from the others. Two POV characters who think in the same register will produce chapters that blur regardless of plot content. The split between public voice and inner voice should also differ per character — some are more honest inside than out; some are *more* performative inside than out; some are the same in both registers.

### Thematic Posture differentiation (Extended Major bios, when the story has a defined thematic axis)
Scan the main-cast Postures as a set. Each major character's Posture must answer the story's organizing thematic question *differently*, and the differences must be produced by character psychology rather than thematically assigned. Test: strip the Posture labels and read only the mechanism and scene-level behavior — would each character still be doing something distinct? If two Postures collapse into the same answer, revisit — usually one needs pushing further toward the pole it's already approaching, or one character's Lie needs sharpening. The Postures together should cover a meaningful span of the axis, not cluster in one region.

### Calibration when information is limited
When story materials are thin, calibrate by tier and type:
- **Major and Supporting bios**: prioritize Psychology and Voice. These carry the most downstream weight. Physical Presence, Social Context, and Story Role can lean on lighter hints.
- **Minor bios**: prioritize Physical Presence and Voice. Skip Lie/Ghost — minor characters often don't have them.
- **Worldbuilding**: prioritize Sensory Palette and Consistency Constraints over History and Cultural Context. The prose agent needs to know how the element *behaves* before it needs to know how it came to *exist*.

Bring the user a small set of choices for the missing dimensions rather than fabricating from genre priors.

## Worked Examples

The examples below illustrate the schemas — what each tier's content looks like when written well. They are reference points for specificity, sensory texture, and craft. Not templates to recite.

### Example: Supporting Bio (~700w) — Marcus Webb

**Quick Reference:** Marcus Webb, 52, Detective Sergeant (Metro Police Homicide), Ensemble Supporting, lives in a two-bedroom apartment since divorce, divorced 12 years, Enneagram 6w5, **The Lie:** "Caring less would protect me — the burned-out detectives had the right idea."

#### Essential Identity

Marcus Webb is a lifer who's spent three decades watching the worst humanity can produce and keeps showing up anyway. He's the lead detective on the Hargrove case, and his arc is modest but real: he enters the story as a man running on professional obligation and leaves it having remembered why he became a cop in the first place.

#### Physical Presence & Sensory Signature

Tall and broad-shouldered, Marcus carries the build of a former athlete who stopped working out around the time his marriage ended but never quite went soft. His hair is graying and kept military-short. Deep lines frame his eyes — decades of too little sleep etched into his face. He wears rumpled suits that were expensive when he bought them fifteen years ago, always with his shield clipped to his belt, and walks with a slight limp from an old knee injury he refuses to get fixed. He smells like black coffee and leather — the interior of his unmarked sedan has permeated everything he owns.

#### Voice & Mannerisms

Marcus clicks his pen when thinking — a rapid, unconscious rhythm that drives his partner up the wall. Rarely smiles, but when he does, it transforms his whole face into something unexpectedly warm. He keeps emotion behind a flat, watchful affect.

**Register & rhythm:** Short, clipped sentences. Working-class direct, Anglo-Saxon monosyllables, with occasional flashes of dark precise wit. The quieter he gets, the angrier he is. **Reference well:** old cases he's worked and the procedural canon of a thirty-year cop; no literary or pop-culture reaching. **Profanity profile:** swears casually among colleagues — "goddammit," "son of a bitch" — but never in front of witnesses or victims' families. **Never-says:** never says "I feel"; never uses therapy vocabulary; never asks his partner how he's doing.

He tells uncomfortable truths when it serves the case and deflects when it doesn't. His tell when lying: he looks directly at you, longer than is comfortable.

#### Psychology & Inner World

**Myers-Briggs: ISTJ.** Marcus trusts process, precedent, and evidence — he distrusts hunches even when his own hunches are usually right.

**Enneagram 6w5 (The Loyalist with an Investigator wing).** His core drive is security through competence and preparation. In integration (toward 9), Marcus becomes genuinely relaxed and trusting, able to let his guard down with his partner and find peace in small domestic moments. In disintegration (toward 3), he becomes image-obsessed and workaholic, chasing clearance rates as proof of his worth.

His conscious drive is closing cases and getting justice for victims' families. What he actually needs is to forgive himself for the ones he didn't close. Under pressure, Marcus freezes — goes quiet, retreats into evidence, and shuts out everyone until he's worked through it alone.

#### Background

Marcus grew up blue-collar in a cop family — his father walked a beat for thirty years and died of a heart attack six months into retirement. That death is his Ghost: it taught him that the job takes everything and gives nothing back, and he's been trying to prove that lesson wrong his entire career. His marriage ended twelve years ago, amicably enough. His ex-wife remarried; they exchange Christmas cards.

#### Social Context

Solidly middle class on a detective's salary. Community college and the police academy — Marcus is well-read from decades of long stakeouts but self-conscious about his lack of formal education. Lapsed Catholic who still crosses himself at crime scenes, a reflex he can't explain and doesn't try to. Politically a moderate pragmatist who votes on law enforcement funding and pension issues; he distrusts politicians of all stripes but reserves special contempt for the ones who use crime statistics as campaign props.

#### Story Function

Marcus is the investigation's anchor — methodical where the protagonist is intuitive, skeptical where they're passionate. His key relationship is with the protagonist, whom he initially treats as a civilian nuisance before grudgingly recognizing their value. He also serves as a mirror: his quiet burnout reflects what the protagonist could become if they let obsession consume them.

### Example: Worldbuilding Full Entry (Location, ~280w) — The Grand Market of Khelvan

**Core Identity.** The commercial heart of the city — a three-block warren of canvas-roofed lanes where every legal good and a fair amount of illegal contraband changes hands. The Market is where the protagonist's investigation begins in Chapter 3 and discovers the smuggling network that drives the back half of the novel.

**Sensory Palette.** Beneath a patchwork of dyed canvas awnings — saffron, indigo, ochre, faded brick-red — that filters sunlight into shifting shadow. The air is thick with competing spices: cinnamon and cardamom from the eastern stalls, woodsmoke from the central grilling pits, the sharp vinegar tang of the pickle vendor near the south entrance. The sound is a constant low roar — bargaining in three languages, brass scales clinking, the ring of the coppersmith's hammer. After dark, oil lamps replace sunlight and the canvas overhead glows like a stained-glass cathedral.

**Consistency Constraints.** Open every day except the three feast days of Avenir; on those days every stall is closed and the lanes are silent. The Market guard ("the Striped Cloaks") patrols in pairs and never enters the inner courtyards uninvited — those are governed by the merchant guilds. Exactly four entrances: Hawk Gate (north), Salt Gate (south), Iron Gate (east), Cattle Gate (west), plus a hidden passage in Spice Lane known only to the smuggling network.

**Physical Characteristics.** Organized by guild — Spice Lane, Cloth Lane, Iron Lane, Animal Quarter — radiating from a central courtyard. The canvas roof is anchored to stone columns that double as bulletin boards. At center, raised on a six-foot dais, is the Weighing Stone where official measurements are arbitrated.

**Failure Modes.** Floods badly in the autumn rains. Fires in the food courtyard are an ever-present danger. The hidden passage in Spice Lane partially collapsed in 1247.

**Story Role.** Where the protagonist first encounters the smuggling network, negotiates her alliance with the spice guild, and where the climactic chase of Act II plays out across the rooftops.
