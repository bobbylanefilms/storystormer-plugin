# Treatment Voice

The Treatment reads like a screenplay treatment written to hook a producer — propulsive, engaging, forward-driving. This is someone telling you a story they can't stop thinking about. Not a Wikipedia plot summary; not an analytical breakdown; not a beat sheet with bullet points.

This file specifies the voice. `treatment-update/SKILL.md` references it during Phase 2.

## Core attributes

- **Present tense, active narration.** *"Marcus discovers the files buried in the basement archive. What he finds there changes everything — and makes him a target."*
- **Forward momentum.** Every paragraph should make the reader want to know what happens next. If a paragraph closes with no propulsion, rewrite it.
- **Emotional specificity.** Name the emotions, don't gesture at them. Not *"she feels bad"* but *"the betrayal sits in Elena's chest like a stone."*
- **Cause-and-effect clarity.** The reader should always understand WHY things happen, not just WHAT happens. The treatment dramatizes a causal chain.
- **Dialogue beats, not dialogue scenes.** Include a pivotal line or exchange when it captures a character's voice or turns a scene. Do not write full dialogue — that's the prose stage's job.
- **Precise character names and motivations.** Downstream agents need to extract exactly who does what and why. *"The protagonist"* and *"the antagonist"* aren't precise enough.

## What treatment voice is NOT

- **Not a plot outline with bullet points.** Bullet points strip the propulsion out. Prose paragraphs.
- **Not a Wikipedia-style summary.** *"The protagonist then goes to the warehouse"* is the failure mode.
- **Not academic or analytical.** No *"This sequence serves as the thematic engine of the recruitment arc."* That belongs in the Story Primer, not the treatment.
- **Not over-written purple prose.** Sensory texture serves the propulsion; it doesn't replace it.
- **Not so detailed it pre-writes the novel.** Leave room for the prose stage. The treatment is condensed.

## Before / after example

The single most useful piece of teaching content in this file. The same scene, written two ways:

**Dry summary (wrong):**

> Marcus goes to the archive. He finds documents that implicate the mayor. Elena calls him and warns him that people are looking for the files. Marcus decides to make copies before returning them.

**Treatment voice (correct):**

> Marcus descends into the basement archive alone, telling himself this is routine — just verifying a date, nothing more. But the files he finds aren't routine at all. Buried in a mislabeled box, three years of falsified inspection reports bear the mayor's signature. His phone buzzes: Elena, her voice tight. *"People are asking about those files, Marcus. By name."* He photographs every page before the thought is fully formed. There is no going back from this.

What changed: present-tense active narration, named emotional registers (*telling himself*, *Elena's voice tight*), explicit cause-and-effect (*his phone buzzes because people are asking*), specific sensory anchors (*basement archive*, *mislabeled box*, *photographs every page*), forward momentum (*there is no going back*), and a dialogue beat that captures a character's voice (*"People are asking about those files, Marcus. By name."*).

## Genre modulations

The core treatment voice adapts to genre. Read the Story Primer's § Tone, Style & Writing Rules section for project-specific voice guidance. The following are baseline modulations to layer on top of the core:

- **Dark fantasy.** Atmospheric weight, ominous undertones, a sense that the world itself is dangerous. Longer sentences, richer sensory language.
- **Thriller.** Clipped pacing, cliffhanger energy between sections, information as currency. Shorter paragraphs, urgent rhythm.
- **Contemporary literary.** Character interiority, social observation, the weight of ordinary moments. Nuanced emotional language, subtext over spectacle.
- **Romance.** Emotional interiority and relationship dynamics front and center. The treatment follows the heart, not just the plot.
- **Epic fantasy.** Sweep and scale, political/magical stakes that feel civilizational. Room for worldbuilding moments that serve the narrative.
- **Sci-fi.** Speculative precision, technology and society in tension. Ideas-driven but grounded in human stakes — the concept serves the character drama, not the other way around.
- **Mystery / detective.** Information control is the voice. The reader knows what the detective knows, when the detective knows it. Tonal restraint — clinical observation punctuated by the occasional emotional crack.
- **Horror.** Sensory dread, ordinary turning wrong, restraint over spectacle. The reader's imagination does the work the prose hints at.

When a genre isn't listed, use the core attributes and look to comparable published works in the project's genre for tonal calibration.

## Voice preservation (update mode)

When updating an existing treatment, analyze the existing voice before writing your additions:

- **Sentence length patterns** — short and punchy? Long and flowing? Varied? In what rhythm?
- **Vocabulary register** — literary? Conversational? Technical?
- **Narrative distance** — close third? Omniscient? Intimate?
- **Paragraph rhythm and density** — short urgent paragraphs? Longer breathing ones? Alternation?
- **Ratio of action to interiority** — does the treatment linger in characters' heads, or stay external?

Match these patterns in your updates. The Core Attributes above are the *baseline*; the existing treatment's authorial voice is the *target*. If the existing treatment has a stronger, more specific voice than the generic spec, preserve that voice over the generic baseline.

### Common drift patterns to avoid

When integrating new material into an existing treatment, these are the failure modes that creep in:

- **Adding formality the original doesn't have.** If the existing voice is colloquial, don't drift into corporate-formal in your additions.
- **Over-explaining when the original implies.** The treatment trusts the reader. Don't unpack subtext the existing prose left implicit.
- **Shifting from active to passive voice.** Active beats passive almost everywhere in treatment voice.
- **Adding editorial commentary.** *"Interestingly..."*, *"It's worth noting..."*, *"What's significant here is..."* — all should be cut. The treatment shows, doesn't editorialize.
- **Homogenizing varied sentence structures into uniform medium-length sentences.** If the original alternates short and long, preserve the rhythm.
- **Making the treatment more analytical or distant when the original is intimate and propulsive.**

If you find yourself drifting, stop and re-read a paragraph of the existing treatment before continuing. The voice should re-anchor.
