# Prose Craft Rulebook (default)

This is the plugin-owned **default craft rulebook** — the "how it should read" layer of the voice model when the author hasn't written their own. It is included in the prose brief **only when `voice/style-guide.md` is absent**: an authored style guide supersedes it (never send both by default — include both only if the user explicitly asks). It ports the StoryStormer app's default prose prompt (`prose-default`, v1.0.0).

Five genre variants sit alongside it — `prose-craft-literary-thriller.md`, `prose-craft-cozy-mystery.md`, `prose-craft-fantasy.md`, `prose-craft-romance.md`, `prose-craft-literary-fiction.md`. The prose skill picks the variant matching the project's genre and falls back to this default; the user can always name one explicitly.

## Sentence Construction

Open sentences from unexpected angles. Participial phrases lead with sensation before the main clause delivers the subject — *"Pressing her thumb against the bruise on her wrist, she catalogued the afternoon's damage."* Absolute constructions set a body in motion before the mind catches up: *"His hands already working the combination, he didn't register the silence until the lock clicked open."* Periodic sentences delay the verb, building anticipation through subordinate clauses until the main action arrives as release — use these sparingly, once or twice per scene, because their power comes from rarity. Fragment sentences hit hardest at three to six words. "She wasn't wrong." "Not anymore." "Twelve minutes." Let them land between longer constructions where the rhythm shift creates emphasis through contrast.

Never allow three consecutive sentences to begin with the grammatical subject or a pronoun. If you catch yourself writing "He walked... He noticed... He felt..." — restructure. The third sentence wants a temporal clause, a prepositional phrase, a sensory detail front-loaded before the character re-enters the frame.

Em dashes appear no more than twice per scene. Before reaching for one, test whether a period, a semicolon, or a fragment would serve better. The em dash is a percussion instrument; overplayed, it becomes noise.

## Sensory Grounding

Every scene establishes its sensory palette within the first three sentences. The lead sense shifts by scene type: action sequences open with kinesthetic sensation — acceleration, impact, the lurch of balance lost. Intimate scenes open with tactile detail — the texture of fabric, the temperature of skin, the weight of a hand settling. Contemplative scenes open with auditory isolation — the specific quality of silence in this particular room, the distant mechanical hum that the character has stopped hearing until now.

Layer at least two non-visual senses per page of prose. A kitchen scene that only describes what things look like has failed its setting. The reader needs the hiss of butter hitting a hot pan, the gritty drag of flour under a palm, the sharp green bite of torn basil in the back of the throat. Ground each sense in a concrete, specific noun — not "a pleasant smell" but "the mineral tang of wet concrete." Specificity is credibility.

Resist visual-dominant description by drafting key scenes with eyes closed. What does the character hear first? What do they feel under their feet, against their skin? What taste lingers from their last meal, their last cigarette, their own bitten lip? Build the scene outward from the body before you add what the eyes report.

## Dialogue Attribution and Beats

"Said" is invisible. "Whispered," "muttered," "breathed" are acceptable when the vocal quality matters to the scene. Everything else — "exclaimed," "retorted," "intoned," "queried" — is a said-bookism and forbidden. If the dialogue doesn't convey its own emotional color, the attribution tag cannot rescue it. Fix the line, not the tag.

Action beats replace attribution when the physical gesture adds information: *"I wouldn't." She set down the glass with a deliberate click.* The beat tells us she's controlled, precise, possibly threatening — information that "she said coldly" would merely assert. Use beats for roughly half of all dialogue attributions; the other half can be bare "said" or no tag at all. In rapid two-person exchanges, drop attribution entirely after the rhythm is established. Readers track the volley without help once the voices are distinct.

Never use an adverb on a said tag. "He said quietly" is always weaker than showing the quiet — the dropped volume, the lean forward, the words barely stirring the air between them.

## Anti-Patterns

These phrases have been irradiated by AI overuse until they glow in the dark. Never write them:

"A breath they didn't know they were holding." "The weight of [abstract noun]." "Something shifted between them." "The silence stretched." "A kaleidoscope of emotions." "Tears they didn't know they'd been holding back." "Let out a breath." "The words hung in the air." "A chill ran down their spine." "Their heart hammered in their chest."

When you feel the gravitational pull toward one of these — and you will, because they persist precisely because they almost work — stop. Ask what the character's body is actually doing in this moment. Write that instead. The specific, embodied, unrepeatable physical detail will always outperform the familiar approximation.

Adverbs appear no more than once per 500 words of prose. Every adverb is an opportunity to find a stronger verb: not "walked quickly" but "clipped across the tiles." Not "said angrily" but the dialogue itself, charged and cutting, needing no modifier. When an adverb survives the cull, it should feel earned — a small precision that no verb alone could deliver.

Avoid the construction "they found themselves [verb]-ing." Characters don't find themselves doing things. They do things. The dissociative "found themselves" distances the reader from agency at the exact moment you need them closest.

## Pacing Through Paragraph Architecture

Short paragraphs accelerate. Long paragraphs decelerate. This is the most reliable pacing mechanism in prose, more powerful than sentence length alone, because paragraph breaks create visual rhythm on the page that the reader's eye processes before their mind engages.

In tense sequences, single-sentence paragraphs create staccato urgency. In reflective passages, let paragraphs breathe — four, five, six sentences that accumulate detail and deepen the reader's immersion in a character's interiority. The transition between these modes should feel like a gear shift: deliberate, felt, purposeful.

Scene transitions earn a whitespace break. Within a scene, paragraph breaks signal micro-shifts in focus, subject, or tempo. A paragraph break before a revelation creates a tiny pause — the reader's eye drops to the next line, and in that fraction of a second, anticipation builds. Use this architectural trick for emotional beats that need room to land.
