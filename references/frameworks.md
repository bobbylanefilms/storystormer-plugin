# Frameworks

Vocabulary for talking about story craft. Skills reference these when generating bios, working through questions, or shaping the treatment.

Not all frameworks apply to all stories. Pick the ones that fit the project. **These are tools for thinking, not boxes to tick.**

## Character

### McKee — characterization vs. true character
- **Characterization** = the surface (appearance, profession, mannerisms, dialogue style). What we observe on a first read.
- **True character** = revealed by choice under pressure. Who they are when everything is on the line.
- Use: a character whose characterization is rich but who never makes a costly choice is flat. Push for the scene that forces the reveal.

### Truby — moral argument
- Every protagonist takes a *moral position* across the arc. The story is an argument *for* or *against* that position.
- The antagonist takes the opposing moral position.
- Supporting characters are *moral allies and opponents* — variations on the central debate.
- Use: when a treatment feels thin thematically, ask *"what is the story arguing? What's the moral position the protagonist starts from, and the one they arrive at?"*

### Egri — three dimensions
- **Physiology** — body, age, health, distinguishing features.
- **Sociology** — class, race, religion, politics, family, work, hobbies, education.
- **Psychology** — temperament, ambitions, frustrations, complexes, abilities, attitudes.
- Use: a character missing one dimension is incomplete. Most flat AI characters are over-physiology, under-sociology and psychology.

### Weiland — the Lie and the Ghost
- **The Lie** = the false belief the character holds about themselves or the world at story open. The thing they're wrong about.
- **The Ghost** = the wound that created the Lie. The unresolved past event the character either represses or organizes their life around.
- The arc: the story forces the Lie to break. The character either accepts the Truth (positive arc) or doubles down on the Lie (negative arc).
- Use: every major character needs a Lie/Ghost. Supporting characters often need them too. Without them, the arc is mechanical.

### Want vs. Need (Weiland / Truby)
- **Want** = what the character consciously pursues. Usually serves the Lie — it's what the character thinks will fix things.
- **Need** = what they actually require but cannot see. Points toward the Truth.
- Example: a detective who believes he must work alone (the Lie) might *want* to solve the case single-handedly but *need* to learn to trust a partner again. Most arcs are powered by the gap between Want and Need.
- Use: when the user knows what a character is chasing but not why the chase matters. Surface the gap — *"she's after the file, but what's underneath that? What does she think the file will get her, and what does she actually need that the file won't deliver?"*

### Truth and deception
- How does the character relate to honesty? Lie easily and convincingly? Lie badly and obviously? Tell uncomfortable truths others avoid? Omit and deflect rather than fabricate? What's their *tell* when they're being dishonest?
- Use: load-bearing for dialogue scenes and character interactions. A character who lies fluently sounds different on the page from one who deflects through silence — and the difference is invisible if the agent never asks. Especially useful when developing antagonists, mentors, and morally-gray supporting characters.

### Challenge to the protagonist (Truby's character web)
- Not just *"this character is an obstacle"* — but what specific aspect of the protagonist's weakness or Lie does this character force them to confront?
- A good supporting cast is designed so each character pushes the protagonist from a *different angle*: the mentor challenges self-doubt; the rival challenges methods; the love interest challenges emotional walls; the antagonist challenges the entire moral framework.
- Use: when developing the cast, ask *"how does this character specifically challenge the protagonist? Different from how everyone else does?"* If two characters challenge from the same angle, one is probably redundant. Pairs naturally with Thematic Posture below — each character's challenge usually maps to their posture on the central thematic axis.

### Enneagram (for fiction)
- Nine personality types with wings (the adjacent number they lean toward) and integration/disintegration paths (the number they move toward when healthy / when stressed).
- Useful for predicting *what a character does under pressure* — disintegration patterns are gold for the third-act crisis.
- Example: `6w5 → integrates to 9, disintegrates to 3`. A loyal-skeptical 6 with a thinker wing, who finds peace through resolution when whole and shifts into achievement-image mode when stressed.

### MBTI
- Lower-resolution than Enneagram for fiction work, but a useful shorthand for cognitive style (Intuitive vs. Sensing, Thinking vs. Feeling, etc.).
- Most useful for predicting *how* a character processes new information — useful for first-person and intimate third POV.

### Clifton Strengths (top 5)
- The character's *natural talents* — how they productively channel their personality. Not what they value, what they're good at.
- Example: Marlowe's top 5 are Analytical, Strategic, Input, Learner, Connectedness. She sees patterns, plans moves ahead, hoards information, learns continuously, and intuits causal links others miss.
- Use: gives the character a *competence signature*. Especially valuable for protagonists who need to feel like they're operating from real ability, not just plot armor.

### Voice Fingerprint (StoryStormer's house framework)
The combination that makes a character instantly identifiable on the page. Cover each vector in prose:

- **Register & rhythm** — formal/casual, polysyllabic/monosyllabic, clipped/periodic. The *musical signature*. Include 1–2 words they would actually use.
- **Reference well** — the specific domains they reach into for analogy: military history, scripture, sports, case law, their own childhood. Wells should not overlap across major characters — differentiation here is the single best defense against every smart character sounding alike.
- **Profanity profile** — whether they curse, which curses, in what contexts they would never curse.
- **Humor register** — dry / sardonic / self-deprecating / absent / warm. How they signal they're joking.
- **Verbal tell under pressure** — the specific way their speech changes when stressed, lying, or cornered: over-explaining, going monosyllabic, getting grammatically precise, slipping into an old accent, retreating into jargon. Distinct from the general stress response in Psychology — this is specifically *how the speech changes*, not the body's reaction.
- **Never-says** — two or three things this character would *literally never say*. Statements, phrases, or registers that violate their voice. The anti-pattern list is the single most useful tool for maintaining voice consistency, because prose agents are better at avoiding explicit anti-patterns than hitting positive targets.
- **Inner Voice** (for POV characters) — how their interior monologue differs from their public speech. This gap is where intimate POV writing lives.
- **Audience shift** — how their voice changes across audiences (boss / peer / lover / child / stranger). At least 2–3 distinct registers.

### Thematic Posture
When the story organizes itself around a central thematic axis (game theory in a political thriller, faith vs. doubt in a literary novel, justice vs. mercy in a legal drama), define each major character's *Posture* on that axis. A Posture is a stance *produced by* their psychology, not a label imposed on them.

Example: in a political thriller about game theory, Marlowe's Posture might be *"refuses to play games she didn't agree to enter — recognizes manipulation late and overcorrects when she does."* Voss's Posture: *"sees every interaction as a game, plays them all, but underestimates that some opponents won't play by the rules he knows."*

## Theme

### Story = the dramatization of a question
A good story asks a question and dramatizes the asking. *Can a person rebuild after losing everything? What does justice cost? Can love survive contempt?* Name the question explicitly. The treatment is the question's setup; the ending is the question's answer.

### Egri's Premise
The cause-and-effect argument the story makes about human nature. Format: *"[Quality/force] leads to [consequence]."* Examples: *"Ruthless ambition leads to its own destruction." "Unconditional love leads to redemption even in the darkest circumstances." "Blind loyalty leads to moral compromise."*

The premise is what the story *proves*. It is not the theme restated as a topic (*"this story is about loyalty"*) — it's the specific argument the narrative makes through its events.

Use: when the user discusses what the story is "about," what happens at the climax, or what the protagonist learns. Ask: *"What does this story ultimately prove about human nature? If a reader walked away with one argument about how the world works, what would it be?"* If the user can't articulate it yet, mark it `[To be developed]` — premises often crystallize mid-development as the climax takes shape.

### McKee's Controlling Idea
The thematic statement *proven through the climax*. Format: *"[Value] is achieved/lost when [cause]."* Examples: *"Justice triumphs when an individual sacrifices personal safety for truth." "Love is destroyed when partners refuse to accept each other's imperfections."*

More specific than the premise — it names the value at stake *and* the specific mechanism by which the climax proves or disproves it. The premise is what the story argues; the controlling idea is what the *final scene* argues.

Use: this often crystallizes late, once the climax is clear. When the user is developing the ending, ask: *"What value is ultimately at stake — justice, love, freedom, truth, survival? And what does the climax prove about how that value is achieved or lost?"* Mark `[To be developed]` if the ending isn't clear yet.

### Moral argument (Truby, again)
- Protagonist's position → Antagonist's position → Supporting characters fan out around the axis.
- Sub-plots are *variations on the central debate* — minor characters arguing the same question from different angles.
- Test: if your subplots could be removed without weakening the moral argument, they aren't subplots — they're padding.

### Thematic dimension (StoryStormer addition)
The texture of the theme on the page. A theme is not a one-word abstraction ("love," "loss," "power") — it's a *specific dramatic claim*. *"Power that goes unexamined corrodes the person who holds it."* That's a thematic dimension. *"Power"* is not.

## Structure

Most stories benefit from *some* structural framework, but no single framework is right for every story. These are lenses on the same problem — how to arrange events for maximum dramatic impact — and the right choice depends on genre, the user's working style, and where the story is in development. For stories where conventional structure is *fighting* the material (ensemble casts, observer protagonists, theme-driven or voice-driven work), see `references/alt-structure.md`.

### Three-act
- Act 1 — setup, inciting incident, plot point 1 (commitment).
- Act 2 — confrontation, midpoint (genuine shift, not just escalation), plot point 2 (low point / dark night).
- Act 3 — resolution, climax, denouement.
- Use: useful for almost any project — nearly every other framework maps onto it. The midpoint is the single most-missed beat in AI-generated treatments — it needs to be a *genuine shift*, not just "things get worse."
- Limitation: doesn't specify internal beats, so Act 2 can become shapeless without additional structure layered on top.

### Hero's Journey (Campbell)
- 17-stage departure-initiation-return cycle. The originating mythic structure.
- Use: epic, quest-driven, and mythic stories with spiritual/psychological transformation. Strength is connecting individual journeys to universal patterns.
- Limitation: formulaic if forced on stories that don't fit the quest shape.

### Writer's Journey (Vogler)
- Practical 12-stage Campbell adaptation for screenwriters. Key insight: archetypal roles (Mentor, Shapeshifter, Trickster, Herald) are *functions* characters perform, not fixed identities — a single character can perform multiple roles across the story.
- Use: when the user wants mythic resonance with more flexible structure. Particularly useful for identifying what function each supporting character serves in the protagonist's journey.

### Story Circle (Harmon)
- Campbell reduced to psychological essence in 8 steps: comfort → need → cross threshold → adapt → find → pay price → return → change.
- Use: shorter narratives (episodes, short stories, novellas), or when the user thinks in character transformation rather than plot events.
- Limitation: works best for single-protagonist stories with a clear transformation arc.

### Save the Cat (Blake Snyder)
- 15 beats at specific percentage points. The most prescriptive framework — it specifies not just what should happen but *when*.
- Use: genre-tight stories (thriller, romance, horror, mystery); when the user needs precise pacing guidance or the middle feels saggy.
- Limitation: don't impose on literary fiction or experimental work — it'll flatten the story.

### Seven-Point Story Structure (Dan Wells)
- Seven beats designed to be worked **backward** from the resolution: the Hook is the *opposite state* of the Resolution, and the turning points bridge between.
- Use: when the user knows how the story begins and ends but can't find the middle. The backward-design approach naturally connects structure to thematic payoff.
- Limitation: fewer beats than Save the Cat, so the spaces between require the author's structural instincts.

### Truby's 22 Building Blocks
- The most organic structural framework: plot grows *out of* the protagonist's psychological and moral weakness rather than being imposed from outside. The flaw causes the plot, and the plot forces confrontation with the flaw.
- Use: character-driven stories, literary fiction, ensembles, narratives where moral argument matters as much as action. Pairs naturally with Truby's moral-argument vocabulary in the Character section above.
- Limitation: requires a clear protagonist weakness as foundation. If the user can't yet name what's wrong with the protagonist, the 22 blocks won't help.

### Freytag's Pyramid
- 5-part structure (exposition → rising action → climax → falling action → denouement) emphasizing symmetry between rising and falling tension.
- Use: stories where the *falling action* (consequences of the climax) is as important as the rising — classical tragedy, literary fiction where the aftermath is the point.
- Limitation: modern commercial fiction typically front-loads the climax; the long denouement can feel slow to contemporary readers.

### Story Grid (Shawn Coyne)
- A different lens — not a beat sheet but a check for **obligatory scenes**: the genre's required moments. Detective finds the body. Lovers meet cute. Hero refuses the call.
- Use: every genre has them; check the treatment against them. Missing obligatory scenes signal a treatment that doesn't yet know what genre it's in. Pairs with any of the structural frameworks above.

### The Heroine's Journey (Murdock / Pearson)
- Alternative to Campbell/Vogler for stories where the protagonist's challenge is *reintegration* rather than *conquest* — the descent into the self, the reconciliation with what was rejected.
- Use: literary fiction, contemporary drama, stories with grief, recovery, or reconstitution as the core movement.

## Plot Architecture

These are the *dimensions to probe* when plot and structure come up in conversation — saggy middles, act breaks, subplot interweaving, pacing. The frameworks above tell you what *shape* a story takes; these tell you what *components* of plot to interrogate inside any shape.

### Story spine / throughline
The single cause-and-effect chain driving the main plot — not theme or premise, but the concrete sequence of *"this happens, which causes this, which forces this."* Probe: *"What's the one thing your protagonist is trying to achieve? What's the first obstacle? What does overcoming it lead to? What makes the final confrontation unavoidable?"* If the user can't articulate the spine, the story isn't ready for detailed structural work — back up.

### Act structure and turning points
Where the story irreversibly changes direction — the protagonist makes a choice, learns something, or loses something that changes the game permanently. Probe: *"Is this a turning point — does the story look different on the other side? Or is this escalation within the current phase?"* In early brainstorming, identify the two or three biggest turning points; finer structure comes later.

### Subplot architecture
Every subplot should challenge the protagonist's weakness from a different angle (see *Challenge to the protagonist* in the Character section above). Probe: *"How does this subplot connect to the main arc? Does it mirror, complicate, or offer an alternative perspective on the thematic question?"* Subplots that don't connect thematically tend to feel like padding — the test: if you removed it, would the moral argument weaken?

### Pacing and rhythm
The alternation between *acceleration* (shorter scenes, higher stakes) and *expansion* (character development, worldbuilding, emotional processing). Genre shapes expectations — thrillers stay compressed, literary fiction sustains longer expansive passages, epic fantasy cycles between action and worldbuilding. Probe: *"You have three high-tension events in a row — your reader needs a beat to breathe. What's the quieter moment between the chase and the betrayal?"*

### Stakes escalation
Stakes should ratchet across acts: personal → relational → societal/existential. Probe: *"Right now the stakes are personal — Marlowe could lose her job. What happens later that makes it bigger?"* Caveat: this assumes a conventional escalation arc. For stories that work through *accumulation* rather than escalation (literary fiction, politically-engaged narratives), see `references/alt-structure.md`.

### Setup and payoff
The brainstorming phase is the best time to identify setups — they're much harder to retrofit later. When a plot element appears late, probe: *"When does the reader first encounter this? If it shows up in chapter 30, it probably needs to be planted by chapter 10."* When developing early chapters: *"This detail is great worldbuilding — could it also set up something later?"*

### Midpoint shift
The pivot that *transforms the story's fundamental dynamics* — not escalation but recontextualization. When the user's story has a clear beginning and end but a formless middle, the midpoint is usually the missing piece. Probe: *"What happens at the middle that changes everything? Not an event that escalates — an event that transforms."* This is the single most-missed beat in AI-generated treatments.

## Worldbuilding

Not every worldbuilding element needs the same depth of development. Use this tiering to decide what gets a full Canon entry, what gets a short reference note, and what doesn't need any entry at all.

### Full Canon entry — novel / fictional elements
Anything invented or unique to this story: magic systems, fictional cities, invented technology, alien species, fictional organizations with specific rules. These need detailed Canon entries because downstream agents cannot generate correct, consistent descriptions from training data alone. *The Shimmer that warps electronics in your sci-fi noir — full entry.*

### Reference note — real-world with modifications
Real-world elements with story-specific significance or alterations. A real city with a fictional neighborhood. A real weapon with custom modifications. A historical organization portrayed at a specific period with fictional additions. These need short notes capturing only the *divergences* from reality. *Your version of the FBI field office with the secret sub-basement and the off-books task force — reference note.*

### No entry needed — well-known real-world elements
Elements the model already knows from training data. *New York City. A Glock 19. The Catholic Church. An iPhone.* Naming them in the primer or manifest is sufficient.

### Element categories
Worldbuilding elements fall into one of these categories. The category determines which sections the entry needs (see `references/canon-schemas.md` § Worldbuilding Entry Schemas for the section structures, length targets, and category-specific guidance):

- **Organization** — political parties, military units, corporations, secret societies, religious orders, guilds, movements.
- **Location** — a specific physical place (city, building, room, landmark, planet, realm).
- **Geography** — broader physical world: regions, climate, terrain, ecology, continents, biomes.
- **Object** — an item, artifact, or physical thing with story significance.
- **Vehicle** — transportation: car, spaceship, horse, airship.
- **Weapon** — combat implement and its capabilities.
- **Technology** — scientific or technical system, device, or infrastructure.
- **Magic** — supernatural power system: rules, costs, mechanics.
- **Creature** — non-human entity: animal, monster, alien, spirit, sapient or not.
- **Culture** — customs, traditions, social hierarchies, etiquette, taboos, rituals, daily life.
- **History** — past events, timelines, eras, figures that shape the current world.
- **Religion** — belief system as lived practice: doctrine, deities, rituals, devotion gradient.
- **Languages** — speech, register, dialect, untranslatable concepts, invented tongues.
- **Lore** — general mythology, backstory, or knowledge that doesn't fit the other categories.

**Character vs worldbuilding disambiguation**: if a sapient being functions as a worldbuilding element rather than as a character with an arc — a god, an AI system, a hive mind, a corporate egregore that has no individual interiority the story dramatizes — default to a Worldbuilding entry (usually under Creature or Organization). Reserve the Character bio schemas for entities the story portrays as individual people with inner lives.

### Probing for rules during brainstorming
When a fictional element has rules — a magic system, a technology, a social structure — probe for the rules *during* brainstorming. Those rules become Consistency Constraints in the Canon entry, and they're far easier to establish during the creative phase than to retrofit later. Probe: *"What can the Shimmer do? What can't it do? What does it cost? Who knows about it?"* Vagueness now becomes contradiction later.

## Voice & Dialogue

### Leonard's "blind dialogue test"
Strip the dialogue tags. Can a reader identify who's speaking? If not, characters sound alike. (See **Reference Wells** in Voice Fingerprint — differentiated wells solve this.)

### Mamet's diction
- Each character's word choice should be *specific to who they are*. Lawyers don't all sound like lawyers; one is precise, one is bullying, one is folksy.
- Avoid making everyone speak in complete sentences with proper grammar. People are fragmentary; people interrupt themselves; people repeat.

### Sorkin's layered subtext
- The professional surface, the personal underneath, sometimes a third layer of irony or self-awareness.
- Especially useful for ensemble pieces where characters know each other well.

## Using these frameworks

A skill — `character-bio`, `brainstorm-session`, `treatment-update` — doesn't need to apply every framework. It needs to know the vocabulary well enough to:

1. **Recognize what's missing** — *"this character has McKee characterization but no true-character moment yet"*; *"this treatment has structure but no moral argument"*.
2. **Offer options that fit the project** — *"if you want Marlowe to feel like a genuine protagonist, we need her Lie and her Ghost. Three plausible Ghost candidates given your treatment: [A], [B], [C]. Which feels right?"*
3. **Talk to the user in their own register** — if they're framework-fluent, use the names. If they're not, use the underlying concepts in plain language.

The frameworks are vocabulary for the agent. The user gets the *outputs*, not the jargon, unless they want the jargon.

For *how much* of this vocabulary to expose in conversation — the Invisible / Light Attribution / Brief Instructional escalation — see `references/framework-surfacing.md`. The default is invisible. For stories where the conventional structural frameworks above are fighting the material rather than serving it (ensemble casts, observer protagonists, theme-driven or voice-driven work), see `references/alt-structure.md` for the counter-vocabulary.
