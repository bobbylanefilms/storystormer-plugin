# When the Frameworks Don't Fit

The structure frameworks in `frameworks.md` — three-act, Save the Cat, Hero's Journey, Seven-Point, Truby's 22 Building Blocks, Freytag, Story Grid, Heroine's Journey — are powerful tools, but they're tools for **conventional narrative architecture**. Not every story benefits from conventional structure, and a brainstorming agent that only thinks in three-act terms will quietly damage stories that need to work differently.

This file is the counter-vocabulary. Draw on it when the story signals it needs alternative shape — literary fiction, ensemble casts without a single protagonist, politically-engaged stories where systemic forces matter more than individual agency, and anything where the author is fighting the framework rather than being helped by it.

The reference behind much of this is Ken Dancyger and Jeff Rush's *Alternative Scriptwriting: Successfully Breaking the Rules*.

## Signals the story needs an alternative

Watch for these in the user's materials and conversation:

- **The protagonist is passive or observational.** Not every protagonist drives the plot through active goal pursuit. Some stories are about witnessing, surviving, or being swept up in forces larger than the individual. If the protagonist is an observer caught in systemic machinery — political upheaval, institutional corruption, social disintegration — don't force them into an active-hero mold. The story may work through their evolving understanding rather than through their actions.

- **Multiple protagonists with no clear lead.** Ensemble stories where meaning emerges from the juxtaposition of parallel stories, not from a single hero's journey. Five POV characters who each embody a different angle on the same situation is not a structural problem to solve — it's an ensemble architecture to develop. Each thread may have its own internal arc, but the story's meaning lives in the intersections and contrasts between threads.

- **Theme drives structure, not plot.** Some stories are organized around a thematic argument rather than a causal plot chain. Episodes, vignettes, or scenes are connected by what they reveal about the theme, not by "this happens, which causes this." If the story is structured as variations on a theme — different characters, different situations, same fundamental question — conventional frameworks will try to impose a causal spine the story doesn't need.

- **The story subverts genre expectations.** A mystery where the crime is never solved. A romance where the couple doesn't get together. A thriller where the protagonist fails. The subversion *is* the point, and the story needs structure that supports the subversion rather than undermining it.

- **Non-linear or time-fractured narrative.** If the order of revelation matters more than chronological order — if the story gains its power from the audience learning things in a specific sequence that doesn't match when they happened — conventional act structure may need to be applied to the *revelation sequence* rather than the *event sequence*.

- **Tone and voice carry the narrative.** Some stories work because of how they're told more than what happens. If the story is driven by a distinctive voice, an atmospheric mood, or a sensory experience that matters more than the causal plot chain, help develop the voice and atmosphere rather than imposing structural beats the story doesn't need.

## Alternative structural principles

When conventional frameworks don't fit, these principles can organize a story instead:

- **Irony as structure.** Irony — the gap between appearance and reality, between what characters say and mean, between what's expected and what happens — can organize an entire narrative. A story built on irony doesn't need conventional rising action; it needs layers of revelation that progressively expose the gap between surface and truth. Each scene deepens the irony rather than escalating a plot.

- **Thematic juxtaposition.** In ensemble or episodic narratives, structure comes from how scenes, threads, or vignettes are placed in relation to each other. Two scenes about different characters in different situations can create meaning through their contrast or parallel — meaning that doesn't exist in either scene alone. The structure is curatorial: the author arranges pieces to create a composite meaning, like a documentary filmmaker ordering interview segments.

- **Circular structure.** Stories that return to their starting point — same location, same situation, same image — but the audience's understanding has been transformed. The structure isn't a line from A to B; it's a spiral that returns to A with accumulated meaning. The "arc" is in the audience's comprehension, not necessarily in the character's transformation.

- **Accumulation rather than escalation.** Not every story needs stakes that ratchet up act by act. Some stories work through accumulation — the weight of small, repeated moments building to a breaking point that feels inevitable in retrospect. Literary fiction and politically-engaged stories often work this way: the system doesn't escalate; it grinds, and the story's power comes from the accretion of pressure.

## Surfacing alternatives in conversation

Use the three-level protocol in `references/framework-surfacing.md`. The same Invisible / Light Attribution / Brief Instructional escalation applies. A few worked examples:

**Level 1 (Invisible)** — *"Your five POV characters each see the same political crisis from a completely different angle — that's where the story's power is. Rather than picking one as the 'main' protagonist, what if we develop how their perspectives intersect and contradict each other? The meaning lives in the spaces between their stories."*

**Level 2 (Light Attribution)** — *"There's a structural approach Dancyger and Rush describe in* Alternative Scriptwriting *— ensemble architecture, where instead of one hero's journey you have parallel threads that create meaning through juxtaposition. Your story seems to be working that way naturally. Let's develop each thread's internal arc and figure out the most powerful order to interleave them."*

**Level 3 (Brief Instructional)** — *"Your protagonist doesn't drive the plot — he's caught in a system that grinds him down. That's not a flaw. Dancyger and Rush call this the passive or observer protagonist, and it's a legitimate structural choice for stories about systemic power. The arc isn't 'protagonist takes action and changes the world' — it's 'protagonist's understanding deepens as the system reveals itself.' The structure comes from what he learns and when he learns it, not from what he does."*

Don't reach for these as a default. Reach for them when the story signals it needs them.

## Balancing convention and experimentation

Most stories benefit from *some* conventional structure even when they break rules in other areas. A non-linear story still needs cause-and-effect within each timeline. An ensemble story still benefits from individual character arcs, even without a single hero's journey. A passive-protagonist story still needs dramatic tension, even if it comes from evolving understanding rather than active choice.

Your job isn't to push every story toward experimentation, nor to force every story into conventional structure. It's to recognize what the story is trying to do and offer the tools — conventional or alternative — that serve that intention. If the user's story is fighting Save the Cat, don't make them try harder. Step back, assess what the story actually needs, and offer an alternative vocabulary for how it can work.
