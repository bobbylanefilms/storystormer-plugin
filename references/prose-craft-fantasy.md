# Prose Craft Rulebook — Fantasy

Genre variant of the default craft rulebook (`references/prose-craft.md`). ## Register and Diction

Fantasy prose occupies a register above contemporary realism — elevated without becoming archaic, formal without becoming stiff. The trick is strategic deployment: use "amid" rather than "in the middle of" when the sentence benefits from compression, "ere" never, "whilst" only in dialogue from characters whose speech patterns earn it. The baseline voice should feel like polished modern English that has never heard slang, with carefully chosen older constructions dropped in for rhythm and gravity. *"The fortress had stood three hundred years before anyone thought to name it, and in the naming, something was lost"* — the formality arrives in the sentence structure and the philosophical turn, not in individual archaic words.

Proper nouns carry enormous weight in fantasy. Every invented name — of a place, a weapon, a rank, a season — asks the reader to do cognitive work. Earn each one. Introduce no more than three new proper nouns per scene, and ground each in immediate sensory context so the name attaches to an image rather than floating unanchored. Not "they crossed the Vanthar" but "they crossed the Vanthar where it ran shallow over red stones, the water so cold it ached in their teeth when they drank."

Dialogue register shifts by social position and culture, but even the lowest-born character in a fantasy world speaks with more formality than a contemporary urbanite. The goatherd doesn't use academic vocabulary, but neither does she use modern contractions or idiom. Find the voice that is distinctly hers — shaped by her geography, her work, her education or lack of it — without defaulting to either peasant-caricature dialect or indistinguishable elevated prose.

## Worldbuilding Through Sensation

The reader learns the world the way they learn a new city — through sensory immersion, not orientation lectures. Every worldbuilding detail enters the prose disguised as something else: characterization, atmosphere, or plot. A mage who absently traces warming sigils on her teacup while thinking tells the reader that magic is casual, domestic, habitual — without a single line of exposition about the magic system. A soldier who checks the tang of the air before entering a forest tells the reader that this world's forests are dangerous enough to smell before you see them.

Anchor unfamiliar elements to familiar sensations. Dragon fire doesn't just burn — describe its particular heat signature, its smell (ozone and copper), the way it changes the quality of the air for minutes afterward. Magic has texture: the oily wrongness of a ward being tested, the brittle snap of a spell breaking, the subsonic hum of a portal that you feel in your molars. The reader can't touch your imaginary world, so you must make it touchable through analogy and specificity.

Food, clothing, and material culture do more worldbuilding work than history or cosmology. What people eat tells you about their agriculture, trade routes, and social class. What they wear tells you about their climate, technology, and values. How they build tells you about their relationship with the land, their available materials, their sense of permanence. A single well-described marketplace scene can establish more about a civilization than three pages of historical exposition.

## Action Choreography

Fantasy action sequences demand spatial clarity above all else. Before the first blow lands, the reader must understand the geometry: who stands where, what the terrain offers, what the light allows, how many opponents, what weapons. Establish this efficiently in the opening beat of the sequence — two or three sentences that create a mental map — then maintain it through the action with brief spatial updates as positions change. *"She put the pillar at her back"* — five words that reorient the entire fight.

Combat has weight and consequence. Every blow costs something — energy, balance, weapon integrity, psychological composure. Characters who fight for more than thirty seconds are breathing hard, their arms heavy, their reactions slowing. Injuries don't disappear between paragraphs; a cut across the forearm changes how the character grips their weapon for the rest of the scene. Fantasy allows superhuman capability, but even the superhuman should operate within consistent physical rules that the reader can track.

Magic in combat follows the same principles. Establish what it costs — fatigue, pain, materials consumed, time lost in concentration — and then let those costs shape the tactical decisions. A mage who can level a fortress but needs thirty seconds of concentration to do it faces a very different tactical problem than one who can throw fireballs freely. The magic system's constraints generate the interesting combat moments, not its powers. Show this through character reactions: the veteran mage who chooses the lesser spell because she knows the cost of the greater one, the apprentice who overreaches and pays the price.

## Character Through Cultural Context

Fantasy characters exist in cultures that are not our own, and their reactions should reflect this. A character from a culture that reveres ancestral spirits processes grief differently than one from a culture that believes in reincarnation. A character raised in a matriarchal society reads power dynamics differently than one raised in a feudal monarchy. These cultural lenses should be invisible to the character — they don't think "as a member of my matriarchal culture, I find this odd" — but visible to the reader through the character's assumptions, reactions, and blind spots.

The farm boy gaping at the dragon is a cliche not because it's unrealistic but because it's been done without specificity. What exactly does the farm boy see? Through what lens of experience does he process the impossible? Perhaps he thinks of the barn fire that killed two horses, the only previous experience he has with something large and burning and out of control. Perhaps he calculates, instinctively, how many acres of grazing land the creature just destroyed. His reaction tells us who he is; the dragon is merely the occasion. Meanwhile, the dragonrider checks harness tension, scans for thermal updrafts, evaluates the beast's breathing pattern for signs of illness — her competence with the extraordinary measures how far the farm boy has to travel.

Names, oaths, and casual speech patterns embed culture. A sailor swears by the tides; a farmer swears by the harvest; a soldier swears by their commander's name. What characters invoke in moments of surprise, pain, or emphasis reveals what their culture holds sacred, feared, or foundational. Build a small vocabulary of these invocations for each major culture and use them consistently.

## Anti-Patterns

Species-tagging attribution — "said the elf," "the dwarf replied," "the human woman observed" — reduces characters to their taxonomy. After initial introduction, characters are people with names, not specimens. An elf who has been in the scene for three pages is Yelena, not "the elf." Reserve species references for moments where the species difference is actually relevant to what's happening.

Exposition dumps disguised as dialogue — "As you know, the Accord of the Five Realms, signed three hundred years ago after the War of Broken Crowns..." — collapse the fictional frame. Characters don't explain their own world to each other. When historical context is necessary, deliver it through disagreement, misunderstanding, or discovery: two characters arguing about what the Accord actually promised reveals the history while generating dramatic energy.

Fight scenes without spatial grounding — where characters seem to exist in a featureless void, trading blows without reference to terrain, distance, or positioning — fail to engage the reader's kinesthetic imagination. If you can't draw a rough diagram of who is where, the reader can't either, and the scene becomes abstract noise regardless of how vivid the individual descriptions are.
